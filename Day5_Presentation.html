THE INSTRUMENT

Meet the
Multimeter

A multimeter is a diagnostic tool that measures electrical quantities. Think of it as a doctor’s stethoscope for circuits. It tells you what is actually happening inside a wire or component — things your senses alone cannot detect.

Digital Display
Shows the measured value. The green LCD-style readout. Also shows OL (overload) and polarity signs.
Rotary Dial
Selects what you are measuring — DC voltage, AC voltage, resistance, continuity. Rotate to the correct position before connecting probes.
Red Probe Socket (V/Ω)
The positive input. Red probe always goes here for voltage and resistance measurements.
Black Probe Socket (COM)
The common ground input. Black probe always goes here. COM = Common = Reference point = 0V.
10A Socket
Used only for measuring currents above 200mA. Move the red probe here for high-current measurements. We do not use this today.

Rotate to the correct setting before connecting probes. Never change the dial while probes are connected to a live circuit.

Batteries, DC power supplies, phone chargers output side. Use for all low-voltage work in this programme.

THEORY ONLY
TASK 1 OF 4

Measure
DC Voltage

Measuring the voltage of your 9V battery. This is the most fundamental measurement in electronics. Every session from here uses this skill.

1
Rotate the dial to DCV. Select a range above 9V — use 20V if available, or the next range up.
2
Insert the red probe into the V/Ω socket. Insert the black probe into the COM socket.
3
Touch the red probe tip to the + (positive) terminal of the 9V battery.
4
Touch the black probe tip to the − (negative) terminal of the battery at the same time.
5
Read the display. You should see approximately 8.5V–9.2V depending on battery charge.
6
Now swap the probes. The display shows a negative value — confirming polarity. Both readings are correct.
EXPECTED DISPLAY
9.12
RED probe → V/Ω socket → Battery + terminal
BLACK probe → COM socket → Battery − terminal
If the display shows a negative value, your probes are reversed. Swap them for a positive reading.
TASK 2 OF 4

Measure
Resistance

Measuring resistance confirms the value of a resistor and verifies connections in a de-energised circuit. It is also used to check if a component is open-circuit (broken) or short-circuit (conducting when it should not).

1
Rotate the dial to Ω. Select a range higher than the expected resistance.
2
Ensure the component is not in a live circuit. Hold the resistor between your fingers — not touching both probe tips simultaneously.
3
Touch one probe tip to each leg of the resistor. Polarity does not matter for resistance.
4
Read the display. Compare to the colour code value. Tolerance of ±5% is normal.
RESISTOR EXPECTED TYPICAL READING
Yellow-Violet-Brown 470Ω 448–494Ω
Brown-Black-Red 1kΩ 950–1050Ω
Brown-Black-Orange 10kΩ 9.5k–10.5kΩ
Brown-Black-Yellow 100kΩ 95k–105kΩ
WHY YOUR BODY AFFECTS THE READING
If you hold both probe tips with your bare fingers, your body resistance (typically 10k–100kΩ) creates a parallel path and skews the reading. Always hold the resistor body, not the probe tips, during measurement.
OPEN CIRCUIT VS SHORT
OL on display = open circuit (infinite resistance, broken wire or component). 0.0Ω = short circuit (the two points are directly connected with no resistance).
TASK 3 OF 4

The Continuity
Test

Continuity mode is the fastest and most used fault-finding tool in the multimeter. Set the dial to the beep symbol and touch the probes to two points — if the path between them is complete, the meter beeps. If silent, there is a break.

1
Rotate dial to the beep/continuity symbol (— usually a diode symbol with sound waves, or ))).
2
Touch the two probe tips together. The meter should beep immediately — confirming it is working.
3
Now test a complete wire — touch one probe to each end. Beep = wire is good. Silence = broken wire.
4
Test a fuse — place probes on each end cap. Beep = fuse intact. Silence = blown fuse.
CONTINUITY BEEP
♪
BEEP — Circuit Complete
Current can flow between the two probe points. The wire or connection is intact. No break detected.
—
SILENCE — Open Circuit
No current path exists between the probes. The wire is broken, the fuse is blown, or the connection is incomplete.
✔
What you can test today
Wire continuity • Fuse integrity • Switch function • PCB track continuity • Your Day 4 plug terminals (Slide 8)
TASK 4 OF 4 — THEORY ONLY

AC Voltage
— Know it.
Never touch it.

Your multimeter can measure 230V AC mains voltage. But in this programme, we do not do this. You need to know what the setting is, what it reads, and why it exists — so you can recognise it and avoid accidentally selecting it.

NON-NEGOTIABLE
Never connect probes to a live 230V mains socket unless you are a licensed electrician with the correct PPE, training, and authorisation. A single touch of a live terminal can cause cardiac arrest. The multimeter can read it — that does not mean you should.
📋
ACV setting on the dial
Usually labelled ACV or V∼. Select a range above 230V (e.g. 600V or 750V) to measure Indian mains.
📊
What a correct reading looks like
Indian mains: 220–240V AC. A healthy socket reads approximately 230V. Significant deviation indicates a supply problem.
⚠
Why it matters to you
A qualified electrician uses this to verify that a socket is live before working near it. After Day 10, you will know when to call one — and why.
230V AC WAVEFORM — 50Hz
DC (YOUR 9V BATTERY)
Constant, steady voltage. Flows in one direction. Safe to work with at low voltages in this lab.
AC (YOUR WALL SOCKET)
Alternates direction 50 times per second. 230V peak. Never handled directly in this programme.
LINKING DAY 4 & DAY 5

Verify Your
Day 4 Plug Top

You wired a plug top yesterday. Today you verify your own work using the multimeter. This is exactly how a professional electrician checks a newly wired installation before it goes live.

T1
Continuity: Live terminal to Live pin
Set to beep mode. Touch probes to the L terminal screw and the bottom-left pin. Expect: BEEP.
PENDING
T2
Continuity: Neutral terminal to Neutral pin
Touch probes to N terminal and bottom-right pin. Expect: BEEP.
PENDING
T3
Continuity: Earth terminal to Earth pin
Touch probes to E terminal and top (longest) pin. Expect: BEEP.
PENDING
T4
Isolation: Live terminal to Neutral terminal
Touch probes to L terminal and N terminal. Expect: SILENCE (no beep — these must not be connected).
PENDING
T5
Fuse continuity
Remove the fuse from its holder. Touch probes to each end cap. Expect: BEEP (intact fuse). Silence = blown fuse.
PENDING
HOW TO INTERPRET RESULTS
BEEP on T1, T2, T3: Wires correctly connected to their terminals.
SILENT on T4: Live and Neutral are correctly isolated from each other.
BEEP on T5: Fuse is intact and conducting.
If T1, T2 or T3 give SILENCE: your wire is not making contact at the terminal. Open the plug and re-tighten that terminal screw.
If T4 gives a BEEP: Live and Neutral are shorted inside your plug — open immediately and inspect. Do not connect this plug to any socket.

DISPLAY LITERACY

Reading What the Display Tells You

The display communicates more than just a number. These six readings each mean something specific.

NORMAL READING
12.45
12.45 Volts DC
A valid measurement within the selected range. The number is the value in the unit shown (V, Ω, A).
OVERLOAD
OL
Over-range / Open circuit
The value exceeds your selected range (switch to a higher range), or there is an open circuit (no connection or broken wire).
NEGATIVE POLARITY
−9.12
Probes reversed
Red probe is on the negative terminal. Swap the probes for a positive reading. Not a fault — just reversed connection.
NEAR ZERO
0.002
Near-zero resistance
In resistance mode: almost zero ohms. Either a short circuit or a component with very low resistance (like a wire or closed switch).
BATTERY LOW
BAT
Replace meter battery
The internal battery of the multimeter is low. Readings may be inaccurate. Replace the battery before trusting measurements.
CONTINUITY BEEP
))))
Path is complete
In continuity mode: the circuit path between the two probe tips is unbroken. The meter both beeps and shows a low resistance value.
COMMON MISTAKES

Six Errors — and How to Avoid Them

Every one of these has been made by someone in a lab. Learn them here so you do not learn them the hard way.

🔌
Wrong probe socket
Red probe in 10A socket while measuring voltage. Meter reads incorrectly or blows the internal fuse.
Red probe always in V/Ω socket unless you are measuring currents above 200mA.
🔄
Wrong range selected
Measuring 9V DC with the dial set to 2V range — display shows OL. Or 200mV range — same result.
Always select a range higher than the expected value. If unsure, start at the highest range and work down.
⚡
Measuring resistance on a live circuit
Attempting to measure component resistance while the circuit is powered. Reading is meaningless and may damage the meter.
Always de-energise before resistance or continuity measurements. Power off, then measure.
🧤
Touching probe tips with bare fingers
Your body resistance (10k–100kΩ) creates a parallel path and gives incorrect resistance readings.
Hold the component body, not the metal probe tips. Use insulated probe handles.
🔋
Forgetting to check meter battery
A weak internal battery gives slightly inaccurate readings — especially in resistance and continuity modes.
If readings seem inconsistent or the BAT indicator shows, replace the meter battery before relying on measurements.
🎯
ACV setting left on from previous use
Connecting to a DC battery with the dial still on ACV. Reading is often 0V or erratic — wasted time troubleshooting a healthy circuit.
Always check the dial position before connecting probes. Make it a habit: dial first, then probes.
