THE SCHEMATIC

A map,
not a photograph.

A circuit schematic is a standardised diagram that shows how components are electrically connected. It tells you the relationships between parts — not where they physically sit on a board.

📷
A photograph of a circuit
Shows physical placement, wire routing, component colours and sizes. Hard to follow electrically.
🗺
A schematic of the same circuit
Shows symbols and connections only. Instantly readable by any engineer or technician worldwide. The universal language.
FOUR RULES OF SCHEMATICS
1
Every component has a standard symbol
Resistors, LEDs, capacitors — each has one universal drawing. Same in India, Germany, Japan.
2
Lines are wires — connections
A line between two symbols means they are electrically connected. A break means they are not.
3
Polarity markers matter
+ and − signs, arrows on diodes, longer legs on LEDs — they all indicate direction of connection.
4
Values are written next to symbols
470Ω, 100µF, 5V — the number tells you the specification of that component in the circuit.
THE SIX SYMBOLS YOU MUST KNOW

The Core Vocabulary

These six symbols appear in almost every circuit you will ever read. Learn their shapes — not their names first.

Resistor
Ω — Ohms
Limits current flow. Protects components.
Capacitor
F — Farads
Stores electrical charge. Filters signals.
LED
Vf — Forward Voltage
Emits light when current flows through it.
Switch
SPST / SPDT
Opens or closes a circuit path manually.
Battery
V — Volts
Voltage source. Long line = +, short = −.
Ground (GND)
0V Reference
The return path. Every circuit needs one.
COMPONENT 1

Resistors

A resistor is the most common component in electronics. It opposes the flow of current, converting electrical energy into heat. You already used one in Day 2. Now you know its symbol and how to read it.

TYPES YOU WILL ENCOUNTER
📏
Fixed Resistor
The small striped cylinder on your bench. One specific resistance value. Colour bands tell you the value. Used in 90% of basic circuits.
🎛
Variable Resistor (Potentiometer)
A rotating knob that changes resistance. Used in volume controls, dimmer switches, and sensor calibration. You will see one in the lab.
HOW TO READ IT NOW
Pick up any resistor from your tray. Find the gold or silver band — that is the tolerance band (always on the right). Read the remaining bands left to right. Use the colour code card on your bench.
COMPONENT 2

Capacitors

A capacitor stores electrical energy in an electric field between two conductive plates. Unlike a battery, it charges and discharges almost instantly. Think of it as a tiny rechargeable bucket for charge.

Electrolytic
Cylindrical, polarised. Has + and − legs. Larger values (1µF–10000µF).
POLARITY MATTERS
Ceramic Disc
Small disc, no polarity. Values in picofarads to nanofarads. For filtering noise.
NO POLARITY
SAFETY — ELECTROLYTIC CAPACITORS
Connect an electrolytic capacitor backwards and it can bulge, leak, or in extreme cases rupture. Always check the + leg (longer leg or white stripe) before connecting. This is exactly like the LED polarity rule from Day 2.
WHAT CAPACITORS DO IN CIRCUITS
They smooth out voltage fluctuations — like a buffer tank in a water system. When the supply dips, the capacitor releases its stored charge to keep things running smoothly. This is called decoupling or filtering.
COMPONENT 3

LEDs
& Diodes

An LED (Light Emitting Diode) is a diode that produces light when current flows through it in the correct direction. A plain diode does the same job but produces heat instead. Both allow current in only ONE direction — like a one-way valve.

IDENTIFYING THE TWO LEGS
+
Anode — the positive leg
Longer leg • Flat side absent • Connect to higher voltage
−
Cathode — the negative leg
Shorter leg • Flat side on LED body • Connect to GND

PLAIN DIODE (1N4007)
Same idea as an LED but produces no light. Used to protect circuits from reverse voltage spikes — common near motors and relays. The silver band marks the cathode (−) end.
COMPONENTS 4, 5 & 6

Controlling the Flow

These three components all do one thing — they control whether current flows or not. The difference is how they do it.

💡
Switch
MECHANICAL CONTROL
A physical lever or button that makes or breaks a circuit connection. You press it, the circuit opens or closes. Simple, reliable, used everywhere from doorbells to industrial panels.
Types on your bench: push-button (momentary) and toggle (latching). Try both — feel the difference in your hand.
📡
Relay
ELECTRICALLY CONTROLLED SWITCH
A small electromagnet that physically moves a switch contact. A small control current (e.g. 5V from an Arduino) can switch a large load (e.g. 230V motor) without direct electrical connection. Isolation is the key feature.
You can hear a relay click when it operates. Listen for it when the lab relay module is triggered during the Day 9 scenario stations.
⚡
Transistor
ELECTRONIC AMPLIFIER / SWITCH
Three legs: Base, Collector, Emitter. A tiny current at the Base controls a much larger current between Collector and Emitter. Used as a switch in digital circuits or as an amplifier in audio. The building block of every microchip.
Your smartphone contains approximately 15 billion transistors. The BC547 on your bench is one single transistor you can hold between two fingers.

DEBRIEF

Common Mistakes — and How to Avoid Them

These are the most frequent misidentifications in every batch. Go through each one together.

📏
Resistor vs Diode
Wrong: Confusing a small diode (1N4007) with a resistor because both are small cylindrical components.
Right: Diodes have a silver or grey band on ONE end only — the cathode. Resistors have multiple colour bands and no stripe. Look at the band count.
🔢
Electrolytic Capacitor Polarity
Wrong: Assuming both legs are interchangeable because "it looks symmetric".
Right: The longer leg is always + (anode). The white stripe on the body marks the − side. Never connect backwards in circuit.
💡
LED Anode vs Cathode
Wrong: Inserting the LED backwards, then concluding the circuit is broken when it does not light up.
Right: Longer leg = anode (+). If the legs are trimmed equally, look inside the LED — the larger metal plate is the cathode (−).
⚡
Transistor Leg Order
Wrong: Assuming the leg order of a BC547 is B-C-E from left to right without checking the datasheet.
Right: Face the flat side of the BC547 towards you. Left to right: Collector, Base, Emitter. Always verify on the component reference card.
📌
Ceramic Capacitor Values
Wrong: Reading “104” on a ceramic cap and thinking it means 104 picofarads.
Right: The code 104 means 10 × 104 = 100,000 pF = 100 nF = 0.1µF. Use the reference table. Do not guess.
🧲
Relay vs Transformer
Wrong: Treating a 5V relay module as an unknown black box and setting it aside rather than identifying it.
Right: The relay module has a visible coil housing and typically a 5V, GND, and signal pin header. The clicking sound when activated is unmistakeable.
