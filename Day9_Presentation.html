STATION 1

Dead LED
Circuit

FAULT SCENARIO
A breadboard circuit with a 9V battery, 470Ω resistor, and red LED was working yesterday. Today the LED does not light at all when the battery is connected. The battery has been confirmed working (9.1V). Nothing has been changed on the board.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Digital multimeter
9V battery (confirmed good)
Component reference card
Spare LED (known good)
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Work systematically from power source to load. Test voltage at battery terminals. Test voltage across the resistor. Test voltage across the LED. Perform continuity test on each wire segment. Test the LED independently with a known-good circuit.
DOCUMENT YOUR FINDINGS
ROOT CAUSE
LED installed backwards (anode and cathode reversed). Current flows through the circuit but the LED blocks it due to reverse polarity. Continuity test: wire segments all pass. LED test independently: LED is functional but lights only in correct orientation. Fix: flip the LED in the breadboard.
STATION 2

Warm
Plug Top

FAULT SCENARIO
A 3-pin plug top from a desk lamp was reported as noticeably warm after 30 minutes of use. The lamp works normally and the fuse has not blown. The plug has been disconnected and is safe to handle and inspect.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Flat-head screwdriver
Digital multimeter (continuity)
Visual inspection lamp
Wiring reference diagram
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Open the plug and inspect visually before any meter testing. Look at terminal screws — are they tight? Is wire insulation touching the terminal or is there bare wire outside the clamp? Check cable grip position. Then use continuity to verify each wire reaches its correct terminal.
DOCUMENT YOUR FINDINGS
ROOT CAUSE
Loose terminal screw on the Live connection. The wire makes partial contact but not full metal-to-metal contact. Current passing through the partial contact creates resistance. Resistance generates heat. The plug warms without the fuse blowing because the current is within fuse rating — just not at a clean zero-resistance connection. Fix: open plug, re-strip Live wire, tighten terminal screw firmly.
STATION 3

Blown Fuse
Mystery

FAULT SCENARIO
A plug top with a 3A fuse connected to a desk lamp keeps blowing the fuse every time the lamp is plugged in. The previous user replaced the blown 3A fuse with a 13A fuse “to solve the problem”. Your task is to diagnose why the 3A fuse was blowing and document whether the 13A replacement is acceptable.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Digital multimeter (resistance mode)
Fuse rating reference chart
Lamp specification label (on base)
Replacement 3A and 5A fuses
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Read the lamp wattage from the specification label. Calculate the expected current: I = P ÷ V = Watts ÷ 230. Compare to the fuse rating. Is the lamp drawing more than 3A? If not, why might the fuse still blow? Check the lamp cord for damage. Measure lamp resistance. Calculate whether the 13A replacement is safe.
DOCUMENT YOUR FINDINGS
ROOT CAUSE
The lamp is rated 700W. I = 700 ÷ 230 = 3.04A. The lamp is right at the limit of a 3A fuse — inrush current at switch-on momentarily exceeds 3A and blows the fuse. Correct fix: replace with a 5A fuse (next standard rating above 3.04A). The 13A replacement is NOT acceptable — it removes protection entirely and allows the cable to overheat before the fuse blows in a real fault.
STATION 4

Cold Joint
Board

FAULT SCENARIO
A PCB LED indicator circuit works correctly when first powered, but the red LED cuts out intermittently — sometimes when the board is touched or flexed slightly. The green LED (switch-controlled) always works. The board was assembled by a previous trainee.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Digital multimeter (continuity)
Magnifying glass / loupe
9V battery + header
Circuit diagram of the board
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Intermittent faults that appear when the board is moved or touched almost always indicate a cold joint. Identify the circuit path that feeds the red LED only. Use continuity mode and probe each joint in that path while gently flexing the board. The joint that causes the continuity beep to drop is your suspect.
DOCUMENT YOUR FINDINGS
ROOT CAUSE
Cold joint on one leg of the 470Ω resistor in the red LED's current path. The joint looks soldered visually but has no reliable metallic bond — only surface contact. Flexing the board breaks this contact momentarily. Confirmation: continuity probe on resistor-to-pad shows intermittent continuity under flex. Fix: reheat the suspect joint with iron for 2 seconds, allow to reflow and cool without movement.
STATION 5

Wrong
Resistor

FAULT SCENARIO
A 9V LED circuit has been built on a breadboard. The LED lights up but is noticeably dimmer than expected — barely visible in a lit room. The circuit diagram specifies a 470Ω current-limiting resistor. Your task is to identify what resistor is actually in the circuit and calculate what the correct value should be for a normal-brightness red LED.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Digital multimeter (resistance & DCV)
Resistor colour code reference card
Calculator or notebook for calculation
Spare resistors (various values)
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
Read the colour bands on the installed resistor. Then confirm with multimeter resistance measurement (circuit unpowered). Calculate the expected current with Ohm's Law: I = (V − Vf) ÷ R where V=9V, Vf(red LED)≈2V. Compare to the normal operating current for a red LED (typically 10–20mA). Identify the correct replacement resistor value.
CALCULATE & DOCUMENT
ROOT CAUSE & CALCULATION
Installed resistor is 10kΩ (Brown-Black-Orange). Current = (9−2) ÷ 10,000 = 0.7mA — barely visible. With correct 470Ω: I = (9−2) ÷ 470 = 14.9mA — normal brightness. The 10kΩ was installed because both it and the 470Ω have a Brown band — a common identification error. Fix: remove 10kΩ, confirm replacement is Yellow-Violet-Brown (470Ω) before inserting.
STATION 6

Reversed
Component

FAULT SCENARIO
The Day 7 PCB indicator circuit has been powered and the red LED lights normally. However, the capacitor body is slightly warm to touch after 30 seconds of operation, which is not expected for a 100µF smoothing capacitor in this circuit. The board was assembled by a previous trainee.
TOOLS AT THIS STATION
Visual inspection lamp
Capacitor polarity reference card
Circuit diagram showing C1 polarity
Desoldering tools (if fix attempted)
DIAGNOSTIC APPROACH
A warm electrolytic capacitor is a strong indicator of reverse polarity — current is flowing through it in the wrong direction, generating heat internally. Disconnect power immediately. Inspect the component visually: confirm the white stripe (negative) orientation matches the PCB marking. Check the circuit diagram for the expected + and − orientation. Document whether the stripe matches or conflicts.
DOCUMENT YOUR FINDINGS
ROOT CAUSE
Electrolytic capacitor installed backwards — the white stripe (negative) is on the positive pad side. The capacitor is passing reverse current, generating internal heat. Left powered, this would eventually cause the capacitor to bulge, leak, or rupture. Circuit still functions because the capacitor is in a smoothing role — but it is degrading. Fix: desolder and reinstall with correct polarity (white stripe to negative pad, confirmed against silk screen).
