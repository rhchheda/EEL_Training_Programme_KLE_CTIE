What You Will
Build Today

A simple but complete LED indicator circuit. When assembled correctly and powered with your 9V battery, two LEDs light up and a switch controls one of them. Every component has a purpose.

WHAT IT DOES WHEN COMPLETE
Red LED: always on when battery connected — power indicator. Green LED: controlled by push switch — lights when pressed. Capacitor: smooths voltage fluctuations. Diodes: protect against reverse polarity.
THE ASSEMBLY RULE

Shortest
Components First

Insert and solder components in order of increasing height. Shorter components sit flush against the board. If you solder tall components first, the board cannot lie flat when you flip it to solder the short ones — they fall out of their holes or sit at angles.

1
Resistors
Lowest profile, bend legs flat to board after insertion
~2mm above board
2
Diodes
Similar height to resistors, polarity must be confirmed first
~3mm above board
3
Capacitors
Ceramic first (low), electrolytic second (taller)
~5–12mm above board
4
LEDs
Height must be controlled manually — bend legs to set height
~8mm above board (standard)
5
Switch & Headers
Tallest, last. Mechanical fit before soldering.
~10–15mm above board
COMPONENT HEIGHT COMPARISON
BEND THE LEGS AFTER INSERTION
Once a component is inserted into the PCB from the component side, gently bend its legs outward at 45° on the solder side. This holds the component in place when you flip the board to solder — it will not fall out.
BATCH BEFORE SOLDERING
Insert all resistors first, flip the board, solder all resistors, flip back, trim. Then move to diodes. Do not mix components across steps — it causes errors.
STEP 1 OF 5

Soldering
Resistors

Resistors have no polarity — they can go in either way. Confirm the value using the colour code before inserting. All four resistors go in before any are soldered.

1
Read the colour code of each resistor. Confirm against the circuit diagram before inserting. R1 & R2 = 470Ω, R3 & R4 = 1kΩ.
2
Insert the resistor body flush to the board. Bend the legs slightly outward on the solder side to hold it in place.
3
Insert all four resistors before soldering any. Flip the board. Verify all four are seated flat.
4
Solder each leg: heat pad + leg 1–2 seconds, feed solder to the joint (not the iron), remove solder, remove iron, hold still.
5
After all joints are made and cooled, trim the legs close to the solder surface with side cutters. Inspect each joint.
RESISTOR REMINDER
No polarity — either direction works. Colour bands face the same direction for neatness. Legs bent flush means the body sits flat on the board surface with zero gap. A gap is acceptable but flush looks professional and is mechanically stronger.
Wrong value = wrong circuit. A 10kΩ where a 470Ω belongs means the LED may not light at all. Check the code before every insertion.
STEP 2 OF 5

Soldering
Diodes

Diodes are polarised — direction matters. Installing a diode backwards will block current in the circuit it is protecting. Confirm the band orientation against the PCB silk screen before inserting.

1
Find the silver/grey band on the diode body. This marks the cathode (−) end. The PCB silk screen has a matching band symbol showing which way it faces.
2
Align the diode band with the band marking on the PCB. Insert gently — do not force. Bend legs to hold.
3
Double-check both diodes before soldering. Ask the person next to you to verify. Two eyes on polarity is a professional habit.
4
Solder both legs of each diode. Same technique as resistors: heat joint, feed solder, remove solder, remove iron, hold still.
5
Trim and inspect. Check the band orientation one final time after soldering — it is easy to verify while the joint is visible.
DIODE POLARITY RULE
Silver band = cathode = the negative end. On the PCB, the silk screen shows a triangle with a vertical bar — the bar end matches the silver band on the component. If in doubt: do not solder. Check with the instructor.
A reversed diode will not damage the circuit immediately — but the protection it provides will be absent. The circuit may appear to work but will fail under a reverse voltage event. Check the band. Every time.
STEP 3 OF 5

Soldering
Capacitors

One electrolytic capacitor on this board. Polarity is critical — a reversed electrolytic capacitor in a powered circuit can bulge, leak, or in extreme cases rupture.

1
Identify the positive leg (longer, or marked + on the body) and the negative leg (shorter, white stripe on body).
2
Match the + leg to the + hole on the PCB (marked with a + symbol on the silk screen). Confirm before inserting.
3
Insert fully. The capacitor body should sit flush against the board, not elevated. Press gently if it lifts.
4
Solder both legs. Use slightly less heat time than resistors — electrolytic capacitors are temperature-sensitive. 1 second heat maximum.
5
After cooling, visually verify: stripe (negative) matches the board marking. Trim excess leg.
ELECTROLYTIC POLARITY IDENTIFICATION
Three ways to identify the negative leg: (1) it is shorter when new, (2) the body has a white or grey stripe on the negative side, (3) the PCB silk screen has a D-shaped symbol with a flat on the negative side. All three should agree.

STRIPE −
Reversed electrolytic = dangerous. When powered, a reversed electrolytic capacitor builds internal pressure. The vent on the top will bulge. In worst case it ruptures. Confirm polarity before powering any board.
STEP 4 OF 5

Soldering
LEDs

LEDs are polarised and height-controlled. The long leg is always the anode (+). Height matters for appearance — decide how high off the board you want the LED to sit before soldering. You cannot change it after.

1
Confirm polarity: longer leg = anode (+). Match to the + hole on the PCB silk screen. The flat side of the LED body marks the cathode (−).
2
Before soldering, test the LED in a breadboard socket with a resistor and 9V battery. Confirm it lights. A dead LED wastes a pad.
3
Insert the LED into the PCB. Hold it at the desired height (approximately 8mm above the board surface for this build).
4
While holding height, solder one leg only. Check the LED is vertical and at the correct height. Then solder the second leg.
5
Trim legs. The LED must be perfectly vertical when complete — a tilted LED catches the eye immediately on the finished board.
LED POLARITY IDENTIFICATION
Three identification methods: (1) longer leg = anode (+), (2) flat side on plastic body = cathode (−), (3) inside the LED dome, the larger metal plate = cathode. All three should agree. If legs have been trimmed, use the flat side or internal plate.
Solder one leg first, verify height and orientation, then solder the second. You have one chance to get the height right — the joint is permanent after both legs are soldered.
STEP 5 OF 5

Switch
& Battery Header

The switch and battery header are the last components — they are the tallest and must sit perfectly square on the board. Mechanical alignment is checked before soldering even one leg.

1
Push the switch into its footprint. It should click firmly into the holes — all four legs must be through the board before soldering begins.
2
Check the switch is perfectly square and flush against the board from both directions. A tilted switch is permanent once soldered.
3
Solder one leg only on the switch. Recheck squareness from above and from the side. Only then solder the remaining three legs.
4
Insert the 2-pin battery header into the power input holes. Polarity is marked on the PCB — + to +. One leg first, check alignment, then second.
5
Final board inspection before power: all joints shiny, no bridges, all component polarities correct, battery header polarity confirmed. Call the instructor for sign-off.
MECHANICAL ALIGNMENT GUIDE
For through-hole switches: look from directly above the board — the switch body should appear to be perfectly parallel to the board edges. Then look from the side — the body should be level, not tilted forward or back. Both views must pass before soldering the remaining legs.
PRE-POWER CHECKLIST
□ All joints shiny and volcano-shaped
□ No solder bridges between pads
□ Diodes facing correct direction
□ Capacitor polarity verified
□ LED polarity verified
□ Battery header polarity verified
Instructor sign-off required before battery is connected. All six checks above must be confirmed. This is not optional.
THE MOMENT OF TRUTH

Power On —
Does It Work?

The instructor has checked your board. Now connect the 9V battery to the header. The red LED should light immediately as a power indicator. Press the switch — the green LED should light.

✓
Instructor has signed off on the board
✓
Battery is 9V and connected the correct polarity
✓
No one is touching any component while connecting
✓
Battery header connection is firm, not loose
RED LED — POWER ON
The red LED lights immediately when power is connected. If it does not — check battery polarity and the red LED orientation before anything else.
GREEN LED — PRESS SWITCH
The green LED lights when the push switch is pressed. Release the switch — green LED goes off. Red LED stays on.
Both LEDs work as expected
Excellent. Your assembly is electrically correct. Move to the quality review on the next slide.
One or both LEDs do not work
Do not panic. Disconnect the battery. Proceed to Slide 10 for the systematic fault-finding procedure. Every fault has a cause.
FAULT FINDING

Systematic Diagnosis

If the board did not work as expected: disconnect battery first. Then work through these checks in order. Each one rules out one possible cause.

CHECK 01
Nothing lights up at all
Test battery voltage with multimeter (should be 8.5–9.2V). Check battery header connections with continuity mode. Verify header polarity matches board marking.
→ Most common cause: battery header inserted backwards, or header solder joint not making contact.
CHECK 02
Red LED not lighting
Continuity: probe from battery + trace to red LED anode pad. Probe from red LED cathode pad through resistor to GND. If breaks found, cold joint in that path.
→ Most common cause: red LED polarity reversed, or cold joint on LED or its current-limiting resistor.
CHECK 03
Green LED not lighting when switch pressed
Press switch. Use multimeter in DCV mode across switch terminals — should read ~9V when released, ~0V when pressed. If not switching: cold joint on switch legs.
→ Most common cause: one switch leg not soldered, or green LED polarity reversed.
CHECK 04
LED very dim
Measure voltage across the LED with DCV. If significantly lower than expected Vf: wrong resistor value (too high) or high-resistance cold joint acting as additional resistance.
→ Most common cause: wrong resistor value inserted, or a cold joint with measurable resistance in series.
CHECK 05
Component gets warm quickly
Disconnect battery immediately. A component that gets warm indicates excessive current — usually a solder bridge between two pads, or a component inserted incorrectly creating a short path.
→ Most common cause: solder bridge between adjacent pads. Inspect under magnification. Use desoldering wick to remove bridge.
CHECK 06
Intermittent — works sometimes
Gently flex the PCB while powered. If the fault appears and disappears: a cold joint that makes contact under pressure. Probe all joints with continuity mode while moving the board.
→ Most common cause: cold joint that looks good visually but has no reliable bond. Reheat and reflow the suspect joint.
🔍 The diagnostic process: Symptom → Isolate the circuit section → Continuity test the suspect path → Find the fault → Fix → Retest. This is the same process used in professional electronics repair.
