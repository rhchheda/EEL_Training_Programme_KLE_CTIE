REWORK DEFINED

Removing
what was
soldered.

Desoldering is the controlled removal of solder from a joint in order to remove a component or correct a fault. It is used in repair, upgrade, and quality control. Every professional electronics technician performs it daily.

🔧
Wrong component installed
Remove the incorrect component, clean the pad, install the correct one. Faster than replacing the whole board.
❌
Cold or bridged joint needing correction
Remove excess solder cleanly, reflow the joint correctly. One desoldering pass saves the component.
💻
Failed component needing replacement
Remove the dead component, clean the pads, fit a replacement. The PCB continues its service life.
⬆
Upgrading a component value
Replace a resistor with a different value, swap an LED colour, fit a higher-rated capacitor — without discarding the board.
SOLDERING
ADDING SOLDER
Apply heat to the joint. Feed solder. Remove iron. The joint bonds component to pad permanently. Forward operation — creating connections.
↕
DESOLDERING
REMOVING SOLDER
Apply heat to the existing joint. Use wick or sucker to remove molten solder. Component releases from pad. Reverse operation — undoing connections.
THE CRITICAL DIFFERENCE
Desoldering requires the same iron temperature and heat-to-joint technique as soldering. The difference is what you do with the molten solder — you remove it rather than add more.
DESOLDERING TOOLKIT

Three Tools. One Job.

Each tool has a specific application. You will use all three today. Pick each one up as it is described.

🧵
Desoldering Wick
COPPER BRAID · 2.0MM WIDTH
A flat braid of fine copper wire coated with flux. When placed on a hot solder joint, the capillary action of the braid draws molten solder out of the joint and into the braid. Best for flat pads and precise removal.
Pull the wick away while still hot. Pulling cold rips the copper pad off the board.
💉
Solder Sucker
SPRING-LOADED PUMP
A spring-loaded plunger that creates suction when triggered. Heat the joint, place the nozzle over the molten solder, press the button — the solder is sucked into the body of the tool. Best for through-hole components with large solder volumes.
Empty the sucker regularly by pressing the plunger without triggering. Accumulated solder inside reduces suction.
🔥
Soldering Iron (same as before)
350°C · FINE TIP PREFERRED
The same iron you used on Days 6 and 7. Re-tin and clean the tip before every desoldering session. A tinned tip transfers heat faster to the existing joint, making removal cleaner and faster.
Use a fine chisel or conical tip for desoldering through-hole joints. A wider tip spreads heat inefficiently and risks lifting adjacent pads.
💡 For most through-hole work on this board: use the solder sucker first for bulk removal, then the wick to clean up residue. The combination is faster than either tool alone.
TECHNIQUE 1

Using
Desoldering Wick

The wick works through capillary action — the same force that makes water climb a paper towel. The flux coating activates with heat and draws molten solder into the copper braid. It leaves the pad cleaner than any other method.

1
Cut a fresh 2–3cm length of wick from the reel. Used wick saturated with solder will not absorb more. Always use fresh wick for each joint.
2
Place the wick flat on top of the joint you want to desolder. The wick must contact the solder, not just the component leg.
3
Press the iron tip onto the wick, not directly on the joint. Heat transfers through the wick into the solder. Hold for 2–3 seconds.
4
Watch the solder flow into the wick — the wick will change colour from copper to silver-grey as it absorbs solder. This is correct.
5
Remove the iron and wick together in one smooth motion while still hot. Do not let the wick cool on the pad before removing.
6
Inspect the pad. It should be clean copper. If residual solder remains, repeat with a fresh piece of wick.
Pull while hot — always. If the wick cools and bonds to the pad before you remove it, pulling it off cold rips the copper pad from the board. That pad cannot be reattached. The board is permanently damaged at that location.
HOW TO KNOW IT WORKED
The pad surface is bare copper, slightly shinier than the surrounding board. No solder blob remains. The component leg can be moved freely in the hole.
TECHNIQUE 2

Using the
Solder Sucker

The solder sucker uses mechanical suction to physically pull molten solder off the pad in one action. It works best for through-hole joints with large solder volumes — resistors, capacitors, headers. It removes solder faster than wick but leaves more residue.

1
Prime the sucker by pressing the plunger down until it clicks into the locked position. It is now loaded and ready.
2
Heat the solder joint with the iron tip for 2–3 seconds until the solder is fully molten and shiny.
3
Without removing the iron, place the sucker nozzle directly over the joint, surrounding the component leg and molten solder.
4
Press the trigger button while holding the nozzle in place. The spring releases, creating suction that pulls the molten solder into the barrel.
5
Remove the iron and sucker together. Inspect the pad. You should see a clean pad with the component leg free in the hole. Repeat if residual solder remains.
6
Empty the sucker every 5–6 uses by holding it away from the board, pressing the plunger, and releasing the accumulated solder into a bin.
WICK VS SUCKER — WHEN TO USE WHICH
Wick: Fine residue, surface-mount style cleanup, single pad work, tight spaces between pads.
Sucker: Through-hole bulk removal, large joints, faster on multiple pads in sequence.
Combined: Sucker first for volume, wick for cleanup — professional standard.
PROTECTING THE BOARD

Pad Care — Six Rules

The copper pad is the most fragile part of the PCB. Once lifted, it cannot be reattached. These six rules protect it during every desoldering operation.

1
Never overheat a pad
Holding the iron on a pad for more than 4–5 seconds weakens the adhesive bond between the copper and the board substrate.
Heat in short bursts of 2–3 seconds. Let it cool for 3 seconds between attempts. Multiple short heats are safer than one long one.
2
Never pull cold wick
Removing cooled wick that has bonded to the pad will rip the copper off the board. This is the most common cause of pad damage in training.
Always pull the wick away while the iron is still on it — in one smooth motion, iron and wick together, simultaneously.
3
Never force a component
If a component does not come free after desoldering, forcing it out mechanically tears the pad with it.
If the component is stuck, there is still solder in the hole. Apply heat again and remove the remaining solder before attempting removal.
4
Add flux before desoldering
Old solder loses flux over time and does not reflow cleanly. Trying to desolder dry old solder requires excessive heat.
Apply a tiny amount of flux paste to the joint before heating. The fresh flux lowers the melting point and helps the solder flow into the wick faster.
5
Desolder one pin at a time
Trying to heat all pins of a multi-leg component simultaneously (e.g. a switch) risks overheating the board and lifting multiple pads at once.
Desolder each pin fully and individually. Alternate between pins to allow cooling between operations.
6
Clean the pad after removal
Leaving flux residue and solder remnants on the pad before fitting the new component. The new joint will be on top of contamination.
Wipe the pad with isopropyl alcohol on a cotton bud after desoldering. The pad should be clean copper before the new component goes in.

IF A PAD LIFTS
Stop immediately. Inform the instructor. Do not attempt to reattach. The board is repairable — the instructor will show you the bridging technique. This is a learning moment, not a failure.
FULL REPLACEMENT CYCLE

Remove.
Clean.
Replace.

The complete rework cycle used in professional electronics repair. Every step matters — skipping pad cleaning is the most common cause of poor new joints after rework.

1
Identify the component to be replaced. Confirm the correct replacement part is available before removing the old one.
2
Desolder all legs of the component using wick and sucker combination. Verify each pad is clean before proceeding.
3
Remove the component by gently pulling upward while the last joint is still warm. Do not force.
4
Clean the pads with IPA on a cotton bud. Wait 30 seconds for IPA to evaporate before soldering.
5
Inspect each pad for damage. Verify hole is clear. Apply fresh flux to the pads.
6
Insert the new component. Confirm polarity for diodes, LEDs, and capacitors. Solder each leg using the Day 6 technique.
7
Test the circuit after replacement. A successfully replaced component is electrically indistinguishable from the original.
CONFIRMING REPLACEMENT BEFORE REMOVAL
Never desolder a component without having its replacement in hand. A board with empty pads and no replacement part is in a worse state than a board with a faulty component. Confirm part availability first.
VERIFYING POLARITY ON THE REPLACEMENT
Even if the original was installed incorrectly, verify the replacement independently using the component reference card — not by copying the orientation of what you just removed. The reason you are replacing it may be a polarity error.
IPA CLEANING — WHY IT MATTERS
Flux residue on a pad is slightly corrosive over time. It also reduces the bond between fresh solder and the copper. A clean pad before re-soldering produces a better joint with less solder than a contaminated one.
COMMON FAULT FIX

Removing Solder Bridges

A solder bridge is solder connecting two pads that should be separate — creating a short circuit. It is one of the most common PCB faults and one of the easiest to fix when you know how.

THE PROBLEM — A SOLDER BRIDGE
Symptom: Device does not work, component gets warm, or circuit behaves erratically. Detected by: continuity test between the two bridged pads (beep when there should be silence).
THREE WAYS TO FIX IT
Method 1 — Wick over the bridge
Place fresh wick across the bridge. Apply iron. The wick absorbs the bridge solder. Remove while hot. Both pads should now be separate.
BEST FOR: small bridges with minimal solder volume
Method 2 — Drag with iron tip
Drag the iron tip sideways across the bridge while it is molten. Surface tension on the tip pulls excess solder away. Wipe tip immediately on brass wool.
BEST FOR: bridges between pins that are hard to access with wick
Method 3 — Sucker on the bridge
Heat the bridge, place sucker nozzle over it, trigger. The sucker pulls the bridge solder away. Follow with wick to clean residue.
BEST FOR: large bridges with significant solder volume
After any method: continuity test between the two pads. Must give silence (no beep) to confirm the bridge is gone.
QUALITY STANDARD

What Good
Rework Looks Like

A reworked board should be indistinguishable from an original assembly. These are the standards that define a successful rework operation. Click each when confirmed on your board.

🔍
All desoldered pads are bare copper — no solder residue
Verify under magnifying lamp. Solder specks less than 0.3mm are acceptable.
⚡
All new joints are shiny and volcano-shaped
Same quality standard as original Day 6 joints. No cold joints on reworked pads.
🧭
All component polarities verified after replacement
Do not assume the replacement matches the orientation of the removed component.
🔋
Board powers up and functions as expected after rework
Connect battery. Red LED on, green LED controlled by switch. Both must function.
🧪
Pads cleaned with IPA, flux residue removed
Board surface near reworked area should be clean, not sticky or discoloured.

WHY REWORK QUALITY MATTERS
In a real electronics repair context, rework must leave no trace. The customer or user should not be able to tell which components were replaced. Visible burn marks, lifted pads, or flux residue are signs of poor rework technique — regardless of whether the circuit now functions.
THE PROFESSIONAL STANDARD
IPC-7711/21 is the international standard for rework and repair of electronics. It defines what is acceptable. The principles in today’s session are directly aligned with that standard.
