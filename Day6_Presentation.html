WHAT IS SOLDERING

Making
connections
permanent.

Soldering is the process of using a heated metal alloy (solder) to create a permanent, electrically conductive bond between a component and a circuit board. It is the skill that turns a loose breadboard prototype into a real, reliable device.

THE ANALOGY
Think of solder as a metallic glue that also conducts electricity. The soldering iron is the heat source that melts it. When cooled, the joint is both mechanically strong and electrically perfect — if done correctly.
📱
Every circuit board inside your phone, laptop, and medical device was soldered — by a machine or by hand.
🔧
When lab equipment fails, a skilled technician uses soldering to repair the connection — not replace the whole unit.
🧪
Custom sensors and measurement circuits for pharmaceutical labs are assembled by hand soldering.
THE TOOL

The
Soldering Iron

Pick yours up. Find each part as it is named.

The Tip
The conical or chisel-shaped metal point that reaches 350–400°C. This is what transfers heat to the joint. Never touch it. Clean it regularly on the brass wool cleaner.
The Barrel / Heating Element
Contains the resistive element that converts electricity to heat. Gets hot — do not hold this section. The handle is the only part you hold.
The Handle
Insulated grip — the only part that stays cool enough to hold. Always grip here. Never set the iron down without returning it to its stand.
The Stand
The coiled metal holder for the iron when it is not in use. Hot iron goes in the stand. Not on the bench. Not on a PCB. Not balanced on another component. In the stand.

CONSUMABLES

Solder, Flux & What Else You Need

Pick up each item from your station as it is described. Know what each does before you use it.

🧵
Solder Wire
60/40 ROSIN CORE · 0.8mm
60% tin, 40% lead. Melts at around 183°C. The rosin core is the flux already built in. Do not use plumbing solder — different alloy, different flux, wrong for electronics.
Hold it like a pencil. Feed it to the joint, not to the iron tip.
🧪
Flux
ROSIN / COLOPHONY
A chemical agent that cleans the metal surface and improves solder flow. The rosin core solder has it built in. Extra flux paste can be applied to difficult or pre-existing joints before rework.
Flux fumes are irritating. Fume extractor must be on before any soldering begins.
🧹
Tip Cleaner
BRASS WOOL WIRE
The coiled brass wire in the holder on your station. Wipe the iron tip through it before and after every joint. A clean tip transfers heat efficiently. A dirty tip wastes heat and creates cold joints.
Use brass wool, not the wet sponge. Brass preserves the tip plating; the wet sponge causes thermal shock.
📋
Practice PCB
THROUGH-HOLE BOARD
Your training board for Days 6 and 7. Has pre-drilled through-holes for components. The copper pads around the holes are what you solder to. You will make at least 6 joints on this board today.
Do not overheat the copper pads. Prolonged heat lifts the copper from the board — a pad that falls off cannot be reattached.
SAFETY CRITICAL

Soldering Safety Rules

1
The iron tip is 350°C. Treat it as an open flame at all times.
A contact of less than one second causes a full-thickness burn. Never rest the iron anywhere except in its stand.
2
The fume extractor must be ON before the first iron is switched on.
Flux fumes are irritating to airways and eyes. Extended exposure to solder fumes is a known occupational health risk. Ventilate.
3
Wear safety goggles. Solder can spit when heated if the surface is contaminated.
A small droplet of molten solder reaching the eye is a medical emergency. Goggles on before the iron is switched on.
4
If you burn yourself — cool immediately under running cold water for 10 minutes.
Not ice. Not butter. Not toothpaste. Cold running water for 10 minutes. Then inform the instructor. Do not continue working until treated.
5
Switch off and return to stand when not actively soldering — even for 30 seconds.
An unattended hot iron is a fire and burn hazard. If you need to step away, iron goes in the stand and the power is switched off.

QUALITY STANDARD

Good vs Bad
Solder Joints

You need to recognise these visually before you make your first joint. Your target is always the good joint on the left.

✔
Good Joint
Shiny, bright metallic surface
Smooth, volcano-cone profile
Fills the pad hole completely
Component leg visible at the apex
Clean edges, no excess solder blobs
❌
Cold Joint (Reject)
Dull, grey, grainy surface
Blobby, rounded, not bonded to pad
Looks bonded but has no reliable connection
May fail under vibration or thermal cycling
Caused by insufficient heat or movement during cooling
WHY COLD JOINTS HAPPEN
The most common cause is touching solder to the iron tip instead of the joint. The solder needs to be melted by the component pad and leg, not by the iron. Heat the joint, feed solder to the joint.
FIRST HANDS-ON ACT

Tinning
the Tip

Before you make a single joint, you must tin the iron tip. Tinning means coating the tip with a thin layer of fresh solder. A tinned tip transfers heat efficiently and produces clean joints. An un-tinned, oxidised tip produces cold joints every time.

1
Switch on the soldering iron. Allow 2 minutes for it to reach temperature. Do not touch the tip during warm-up.
2
Once hot, wipe the tip through the brass wool cleaner 3–4 times to remove oxidation.
3
Touch solder wire to the clean tip. It should melt immediately and coat the tip in a shiny silver layer.
4
Wipe again through brass wool. The tip should now be shiny silver-grey — not black or dark brown.
5
Re-tin between every 2–3 joints. A dull tip = less heat transfer = cold joints. Tin, clean, repeat.
HOT TIP — 350°C
After tinning, your tip should look like this — a bright silver cone, not dark or black. If it is dark, wipe through brass wool and re-tin immediately.
BEFORE
Dark, oxidised, poor heat transfer
AFTER
Bright silver, excellent heat transfer
GUIDED FIRST JOINT

Your First
Solder Joint

THE GOLDEN RULE — NEVER FORGET THIS
Heat the joint (pad + component leg together). Then feed solder to the joint. Never feed solder to the iron. The solder must be melted by the joint itself, not by the iron tip.
1
Insert a resistor into two adjacent holes on your practice PCB. Bend the legs slightly to hold it in place. Flip the board so the legs face up.
2
Clean and re-tin the tip. Then place the tip so it touches both the pad and the component leg simultaneously — at the base of the leg.
3
Hold for 1–2 seconds to heat both surfaces. Then bring the solder wire in from the opposite side of the iron.
4
Feed a small amount of solder. You will see it flow into the joint. Remove the solder wire first, then the iron.
5
Do not move the component for 3–5 seconds while the joint cools. Movement during cooling creates a cold joint.
6
Trim the excess component leg with side cutters. Inspect. Is it shiny? Volcano-shaped? Good joint.

Total time per joint: approximately 5–8 seconds. Any longer risks lifting the copper pad from the PCB.
SELF-ASSESSMENT

Inspect Your
First Joint

Put the PCB under the magnifying lamp or hold it at arm's length with good lighting. Answer each question honestly. Click each row when it passes.

1
Is the surface shiny?
A good joint reflects light clearly. A dull, grey or grainy surface indicates a cold joint — reheat and reflow.
2
Is the profile volcano-shaped?
Solder should taper from the pad up to the component leg — like a concave cone. A ball shape means too much solder.
3
Is the component leg visible at the top?
You should see the wire or leg poking through the centre. If completely buried, you may have too much solder.
4
No bridges or spills to adjacent pads?
Solder must stay on its pad. Any bridge to a neighbouring pad creates a short circuit.
5
Does it pass the tug test?
Gently tug the component leg. A good joint will not move. If the component shifts, the joint did not bond properly.
IF YOUR JOINT FAILS A CHECK
Do not panic. Heat the joint again with the iron (1–2 seconds) and allow it to reflow. Remove the iron and hold still. If the joint is blobby, touch desoldering wick to it while hot to remove excess solder. Then re-solder cleanly.

0 / 5 checks passed — keep assessing

PRACTICE

Now Make
Five More

One good joint proves you can do it. Five good joints starts building the muscle memory. By the end of this session you should have at least six clean, shiny, volcano-shaped joints on your practice PCB.

6
minimum joints by end of session — at least 4 must pass your own quality check. The instructor will inspect each one.
🔥
Focus: Timing
1–2 seconds heat, 1 second solder, remove solder, remove iron, hold 3 seconds. Count it out loud if it helps.
🔍
Focus: Where the solder goes
The solder wire tip must touch the pad-and-leg junction, not the iron. If solder only flows on the iron tip, you are feeding it wrong.
✋
Focus: Stillness during cooling
Do not touch, blow on, or move the joint while it is cooling. Even tiny movement creates a cold joint that looks fine but will fail later.
