CONCEPT 1 OF 3

Voltage
— The Pressure

Voltage is the electrical pressure that pushes electrons around a circuit. Without it, nothing moves. With too much of it, things break. With just the right amount, work gets done.

V
Volts — named after Alessandro Volta Measured with a voltmeter or multimeter set to DCV / ACV
1.5 VAA battery — inside your TV remote
9 VPP3 battery — what you will use today in this lab
12 VCar battery — enough to cause a serious shock
230 VMains supply (India) — wall socket — potentially lethal
CONCEPT 2 OF 3

Current
— The Flow

Current is the actual movement of electrons through a conductor. Voltage creates the pressure; current is what actually flows as a result. No current = no work done.

A
Amperes (Amps) — named after André Ampère Small currents measured in milliamps (mA). 1A = 1000 mA
DC — DIRECT CURRENT
Flows in one direction only. Batteries. Your 9V circuit today. Predictable and safe to work with at low voltages.
AC — ALTERNATING CURRENT
Reverses direction 50 times per second (50 Hz in India). Mains supply. Never handle live AC in this programme.
CONCEPT 3 OF 3

Resistance
— The Friction

Resistance is how much a component opposes the flow of current. It converts electrical energy into heat or light. Without resistance, current would flow uncontrolled and destroy everything in its path.

Ω
Ohms — named after Georg Simon Ohm Higher value = more resistance = less current flows
WHY YOUR LED NEEDS A RESISTOR
An LED (red) needs about 2V and 20mA to shine. Your 9V battery would push far too much current through it — the LED would burn out in seconds. A 470Ω resistor limits the current to a safe level and the LED lives happily.
470Ω
The resistor in today’s circuit. Limits current to ~14 mA from 9V. The LED shines safely.
1kΩ
1,000 ohms. Higher resistance means less current (9 mA). The LED will be dimmer. You will compare both in Slide 10.
10kΩ
10,000 ohms. Very low current (0.9 mA). The LED barely glows — but it is protected completely.
THE FORMULA

Ohm’s Law —
The One Formula
You Need

Three quantities. One relationship. If you know any two, you can always calculate the third. This is the foundation of all electrical work.

V = I × R
Voltage = Current × Resistance — to find Voltage
I = V ÷ R
Current = Voltage ÷ Resistance — to find Current
R = V ÷ I
Resistance = Voltage ÷ Current — to find Resistance
WORKED EXAMPLE — TODAY’S CIRCUIT
V = 9V    R = 470Ω
I = V ÷ R = 9 ÷ 470 = 0.019 A = 19 mA
The LED receives 19 milliamps — within its safe range.

HANDS-ON TASK

Build Your
First Circuit

Everything on your bench is exactly what you need. Follow each step. Do not rush. When the LED lights up, that is Ohm’s Law working in your hands.

1
Place the breadboard flat on the bench. Orient it so the centre groove runs horizontally.
2
Insert the 470Ω resistor across the centre groove — one leg each side.
3
Insert the LED — longer leg (anode +) in the same column as one resistor leg.
4
Connect a red jumper wire from the 9V battery + terminal to the free resistor leg.
5
Connect a black jumper wire from the LED’s short leg (cathode −) back to the battery − terminal.
6
Connect the battery. The LED should light up. If it does not — check LED polarity first.

When correctly wired, the LED glows. That is Ohm’s Law in action — 9V ÷ 470Ω = 19 mA through the LED.

WHY DID IT LIGHT UP?
The 9V battery created enough pressure (voltage) to push current through the resistor and LED. The LED converts current into light — that is its only job.
WHAT DID THE RESISTOR DO?
Without it, the battery would push ~19A through the LED — destroying it in milliseconds. The 470Ω resistor limited current to a safe 19mA. It was the bodyguard.
WHAT IS OHM’S LAW HERE?
I = V ÷ R = 9 ÷ 470 = 0.019 A (19 mA). You predicted the current before you built the circuit. The circuit confirmed it. That is engineering.
EXPERIMENT

Swap the
Resistor.
Observe.

You have a 1kΩ resistor on your bench. Swap it for the 470Ω one. Watch what happens to the LED brightness. Then calculate why using Ohm’s Law.

1
Remove the 470Ω resistor from the breadboard without disconnecting anything else.
2
Insert the 1kΩ resistor in the same holes. Reconnect the battery.
3
Observe: is the LED brighter or dimmer? Why? Write your prediction before reconnecting.
4
Calculate I = 9 ÷ 1000 = 9 mA. Compare to the 19 mA with 470Ω. Does the brightness match?

THE INSIGHT

You just controlled the brightness of a light using nothing but Ohm’s Law and a component swap. This is how every dimmer switch, every LED driver, and every variable power supply in the world works.
